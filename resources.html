Understanding Plans

PPO vs HMO: What's the Difference?

When shopping for health insurance, two of the most common plan types you will encounter are PPO (Preferred Provider Organization) and HMO (Health Maintenance Organization). Both provide medical coverage, but they differ significantly in how you access care, choose providers, and manage costs. Understanding these differences is essential to selecting the plan that best fits your lifestyle and health needs.

PPO Plans: Flexibility and Freedom

PPO plans are built around flexibility. With a PPO, you can see any doctor or specialist without needing a referral from a primary care physician. This means if you want to visit a dermatologist, orthopedic surgeon, or any other specialist, you can schedule an appointment directly. PPO networks are typically larger and often extend nationwide, making them an excellent choice for people who travel frequently or live in rural areas where provider options may be limited.

PPO plans also allow you to see out-of-network providers, although at a higher cost. This safety net ensures you always have access to care, even if your preferred doctor is not within the plan's network. Monthly premiums for PPO plans tend to be higher than HMO plans, but many people find the added flexibility well worth the investment.

HMO Plans: Structure and Savings

HMO plans work within a more structured framework. You are required to choose a primary care physician (PCP) who acts as your main point of contact for all medical needs. If you need to see a specialist, your PCP must provide a referral first. This gatekeeper model helps control costs but can add an extra step when you need specialized care. HMO networks are typically smaller and regionally focused, which means provider choices may be more limited.

The trade-off for this structure is lower monthly premiums and often lower out-of-pocket costs. For individuals and families who are generally healthy and comfortable working within a defined network, HMO plans can be a cost-effective option.

Which Is Right for You?

For many self-employed individuals and families, PPO plans offer the freedom to see the doctors they want without jumping through hoops. If you value the ability to choose your providers, need access to a broad network, or want the peace of mind of out-of-network coverage, a PPO is likely the better fit. If you are focused primarily on keeping monthly costs low and are comfortable with a more managed approach to care, an HMO may serve you well.

  • Choose PPO if: You want provider flexibility, no referral requirements, and a larger network
  • Choose HMO if: You prefer lower premiums and are comfortable with a designated primary care physician
  • Consider your health needs: Frequent specialist visits favor PPO; routine care may be fine with HMO

Self-Employed

Health Insurance for the Self-Employed

Being self-employed comes with incredible freedom, but it also means you are responsible for securing your own health insurance. Without an employer-sponsored plan, navigating the world of health coverage can feel overwhelming. The good news is that there are more options than you might think, and many self-employed individuals end up with better coverage at a lower cost than they expected.

The Unique Challenges of Self-Employment

Self-employed individuals face several challenges when it comes to health insurance. Income can fluctuate from month to month, making it difficult to commit to a fixed premium. There is no HR department to guide you through enrollment or answer questions. And marketplace plans, while available, often come with limited networks that may not include your preferred doctors or local providers, especially in rural areas like the Texas Panhandle.

Your Coverage Options

As a self-employed individual, you generally have four main paths to health coverage:

  • ACA Marketplace Plans: Government-subsidized plans with guaranteed issue, regardless of health status. Best for those with lower incomes who qualify for premium tax credits.
  • Private PPO Plans: Custom plans that often provide broader networks, greater flexibility, and can be 20-60% cheaper than marketplace options for healthy individuals.
  • Health Sharing Ministries: Community-based programs where members share medical expenses. Lower monthly costs but different from traditional insurance.
  • Short-Term Plans: Temporary coverage for gaps between plans or transitional periods. Limited benefits but affordable for bridge coverage.

Why Custom PPO Plans Are Popular with the Self-Employed

Many self-employed clients find that custom PPO plans provide the best combination of coverage, flexibility, and affordability. These plans offer access to large nationwide networks, include telemedicine services, and do not require referrals to see specialists. For healthy individuals who do not qualify for significant marketplace subsidies, private PPO plans can result in substantial savings while providing equal or better coverage.

Key Factors to Consider

When evaluating your options, consider your monthly budget, how often you visit the doctor, any ongoing medications or treatments, and whether your preferred providers are in network. A licensed agent can help you compare plans side by side and find the coverage that aligns with your specific needs and financial situation.

Plan Basics

How to Choose the Right Deductible

Your deductible is one of the most important factors in your health insurance plan. It is the amount you pay out of pocket for covered medical services before your insurance begins to pay its share. Choosing the right deductible involves balancing your monthly premium against the financial risk you are willing to accept when you need care.

How Deductibles Work

When you receive medical care, you pay the full cost until you have met your deductible for the year. After that, your insurance kicks in and typically covers a percentage of costs (often 70-80%), while you pay the remaining portion (coinsurance) until you reach your out-of-pocket maximum. Some services, like preventive care and telemedicine, may be covered before the deductible is met.

Lower Deductible vs. Higher Deductible

A lower deductible means your insurance starts paying sooner, which reduces your financial exposure when you need care. The trade-off is a higher monthly premium. This option tends to work well for families with young children, individuals with chronic conditions, or anyone who visits the doctor regularly and wants more predictable costs.

A higher deductible results in a lower monthly premium, keeping more money in your pocket each month. However, if you do need significant medical care, you will pay more out of pocket before coverage kicks in. This option suits people who are generally healthy, rarely visit the doctor, and prefer to keep monthly expenses low while accepting some risk.

Finding the Right Balance

There is no one-size-fits-all answer. The right deductible depends on your specific situation. Consider these factors:

  • Doctor visit frequency: Regular visits favor a lower deductible
  • Ongoing medications: Prescription needs can add up quickly with a high deductible
  • Family health needs: Children and family members with known health needs may benefit from lower deductibles
  • Emergency savings: If you have savings to cover unexpected costs, a higher deductible may make sense
  • Monthly budget: Can you comfortably afford the higher premium that comes with a lower deductible?

A Practical Approach

One helpful exercise is to estimate your total annual healthcare costs under each scenario. Add up 12 months of premiums plus your expected out-of-pocket spending. Often, a mid-range deductible provides the best overall value, giving you reasonable monthly costs without excessive exposure if something unexpected happens. Working with an agent makes this analysis straightforward and ensures you are comparing apples to apples.

Comparing Options

Marketplace vs Private Health Insurance

One of the most common questions people ask when shopping for health insurance is whether they should use the government marketplace or explore private options. Both paths have legitimate advantages, and the best choice depends on your income, health status, provider preferences, and budget. Understanding the differences will help you make a confident, informed decision.

Marketplace (ACA) Plans

The Health Insurance Marketplace, established under the Affordable Care Act, offers standardized plans in metal tiers (Bronze, Silver, Gold, Platinum). A key advantage is guaranteed issue, meaning you cannot be denied coverage based on pre-existing conditions. If your household income falls within certain thresholds, you may qualify for premium tax credits that significantly reduce your monthly cost.

However, marketplace plans come with trade-offs. Networks are often narrower, particularly in rural areas. Premiums can be high for those who do not qualify for subsidies, and deductibles on lower-tier plans can reach several thousand dollars. Open enrollment is limited to a specific window each year unless you experience a qualifying life event.

Private Health Insurance Plans

Private plans, including custom PPO options, operate outside the ACA marketplace. They often feature larger provider networks, more flexible plan designs, and competitive pricing for individuals who are in good health. Many private PPO plans can save you 20-60% compared to marketplace premiums, especially if you do not qualify for subsidies.

The primary distinction is that private plans are medically underwritten. This means the insurance company reviews your health history during the application process to determine eligibility and pricing. For healthy individuals and families, this underwriting process typically results in favorable rates. Some pre-existing conditions may affect eligibility, which is why having a knowledgeable agent to guide you through the process is valuable.

Key Differences at a Glance

  • Subsidies: Available on marketplace; not available on private plans
  • Medical underwriting: Not required for marketplace; required for private plans
  • Network size: Often narrower on marketplace; typically broader on private PPO plans
  • Enrollment periods: Limited open enrollment for marketplace; more flexible for private plans
  • Cost: Marketplace can be cheaper with subsidies; private is often cheaper without them
  • Customization: Standardized tiers on marketplace; more tailored options with private plans

Making the Right Choice

If you qualify for significant subsidies and have pre-existing conditions that might affect underwriting, the marketplace may be your best option. If you are in good health, do not qualify for subsidies, and want greater flexibility in choosing providers, a private PPO plan is likely to offer better value. Many people are surprised to learn how much they can save with a private plan while gaining access to a larger network of doctors and hospitals.

Small Business

Health Coverage for Small Business Owners

As a small business owner, providing health coverage for yourself, your family, and your team is one of the most important investments you can make. Good health benefits help attract and retain quality employees, protect your family, and give you peace of mind. The challenge is finding coverage that is both comprehensive and affordable when traditional group plans may not be a practical fit.

Beyond Traditional Group Plans

Traditional group health insurance plans typically require a minimum number of employees and can be expensive for small teams. The administrative burden of managing a group plan can also be significant for business owners who are already wearing multiple hats. Fortunately, there are alternatives that provide excellent coverage without the complexity and overhead of a traditional group arrangement.

Custom PPO Plans for Small Businesses

Individual custom PPO plans offer a flexible alternative for small business owners. Rather than purchasing a single group plan, each person can be enrolled in a plan tailored to their specific needs. This approach offers several advantages:

  • Customizable coverage: Each employee or family member can have a plan designed for their unique health needs and budget
  • No minimum employee requirements: Individual plans work whether you have one employee or twenty
  • Nationwide networks: Large PPO networks give employees access to providers across the country
  • Cost control: Choose different coverage levels for different team members based on their needs
  • Simplified administration: Less paperwork and management compared to traditional group plans

Tax Advantages

Small business owners may be able to deduct health insurance premiums as a business expense. Self-employed individuals can often deduct their own premiums on their personal tax return. The specific tax benefits depend on your business structure and situation, so it is worth discussing with your accountant or tax advisor to maximize your savings.

Whether you are a sole proprietor looking for individual coverage or a growing business wanting to offer benefits to your team, there is a solution that fits.

What Does It Mean to Be Medically Underwritten?

If you are considering a private health insurance plan, you will likely encounter the term "medically underwritten." This is one of the most significant differences between private plans and ACA marketplace plans, and understanding what it means will help you navigate the application process with confidence.

The Basics of Medical Underwriting

Medical underwriting is the process by which an insurance company evaluates your health history to determine whether you qualify for coverage and at what price. Unlike ACA marketplace plans, which accept all applicants regardless of health status, medically underwritten plans use your health information to assess risk and set premiums accordingly.

The process typically involves completing a health questionnaire that asks about your current health, medical history, medications, and any diagnosed conditions. In some cases, the insurer may request additional medical records or information from your physician. The goal is to accurately assess your health profile so the plan can be priced appropriately.

How It Affects Your Premiums

For individuals in good health, medical underwriting often works in your favor. Because the insurer has assessed your health status, they can offer lower premiums than they would in a guaranteed-issue environment where everyone pays the same rate regardless of health. This is a primary reason why private PPO plans can be significantly more affordable than marketplace plans for healthy individuals and families.

What Conditions May Affect Eligibility

Certain health conditions may affect your ability to qualify for a medically underwritten plan. The specific criteria vary by insurer and plan, but conditions that involve ongoing treatment, significant medications, or recent hospitalizations may be considered. It is important to note that not all health conditions disqualify you, and many common conditions are perfectly acceptable. This is where having an experienced agent makes a real difference.

Telehealth

MD Live & Teledoc Services

Telemedicine has changed the way people access healthcare, making it easier and more convenient than ever to see a doctor. Many custom PPO plans include built-in telemedicine services through providers like MD Live and Teladoc, giving you access to licensed physicians 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, from virtually anywhere.

What Is Telemedicine?

Telemedicine allows you to have a medical consultation with a licensed doctor via phone call or video chat. Instead of driving to a clinic, sitting in a waiting room, and taking time off work, you can connect with a physician from your home, office, or wherever you happen to be. The doctor can evaluate your symptoms, provide a diagnosis, prescribe medications, and recommend follow-up care, all without an in-person visit.

Common Conditions Treated

Telemedicine is ideal for a wide range of non-emergency medical issues. Doctors can treat and prescribe medications for conditions including:

  • Cold and flu symptoms
  • Sinus infections and allergies
  • Sore throat, cough, and congestion
  • Skin rashes and minor infections
  • Urinary tract infections
  • Eye infections (pink eye)
  • Minor aches and pains
  • Mental health support and counseling

Benefits of Telemedicine

The convenience factor alone makes telemedicine valuable, but the benefits go further. Visits are typically much less expensive than an office visit or urgent care trip. You avoid exposure to other sick patients in a waiting room. For parents with young children, self-employed individuals who cannot afford to miss work, or anyone living in a rural area with limited nearby providers, telemedicine is a practical and cost-effective way to get care quickly.

Mental Health Support

Many telemedicine platforms also offer mental health services, connecting you with licensed therapists and counselors via video sessions. This makes mental healthcare more accessible, especially for those who might feel uncomfortable visiting a therapist in person or who live in areas where mental health providers are scarce. Sessions can be scheduled at convenient times, including evenings and weekends.

Included with Many Plans

One of the advantages of custom PPO plans is that telemedicine is often included as a standard benefit, sometimes at no additional cost per visit. This means you can use the service as often as needed without worrying about copays adding up. When you are evaluating plan options, ask about the telemedicine benefits included, as it can be a significant source of savings and convenience throughout the year.
